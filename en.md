# Beyond Hallucination: AI Dialects and the Formation of Private Standards of Judgment

## A Conceptual Report on Private Language and Judgment Drift in Long-Term Human-AI Interaction

## Abstract

Discussions of large language model risks often focus on hallucination, factual errors, plagiarism, bias, and low-quality AI-generated output. However, long-term interaction with AI systems may produce a subtler and more difficult-to-detect risk. Through repeated interaction, a user and an AI system may develop private terminology, metaphors, abbreviations, implicit assumptions, and local standards of adequacy.

This report proposes the working concepts of **AI dialect**, **linguistic dialect**, **judgment dialect**, and **private standards of judgment**. The term AI dialect does not refer to a full sociolinguistic dialect. Rather, it refers to a personalized working register that emerges within long-term human-AI interaction. A linguistic dialect refers to private or semi-private meanings, terms, and expressions formed in the human-AI loop. A judgment dialect refers to private standards of adequacy, methodological assumptions, and criteria of validity that gradually become naturalized through repeated interaction.

This risk differs from hallucination. Hallucination produces false claims. AI dialect produces private meanings. Judgment dialect produces private standards of adequacy. Therefore, fact-checking alone is not sufficient. Future AI agents may need term grounding, register drift detection, assumption provenance tracking, and consensus boundary auditing.

---

## 1. Introduction

Current discussions of AI risk tend to focus on visible failures: fabricated references, incorrect factual claims, polished but shallow writing, plagiarism, bias, and other forms of low-quality AI-generated output. These problems are serious. Yet long-term AI use may create another layer of risk that is less visible.

When a user repeatedly interacts with an AI system, the two may develop shared phrases, metaphors, concept boundaries, project labels, and standards of adequacy. At first, these may be temporary expressions or convenient working terms. Over time, however, repeated use can make them feel natural. The user may then lose track of whether a term came from the literature, from the AI, from the user’s own language, or from a local agreement formed during the conversation.

This report calls this phenomenon **AI dialect**. The term is metaphorical. It does not imply a full dialect in the sociolinguistic sense. It refers to a private or semi-private working register formed within a personalized human-AI relationship.

Two layers are especially important.

The first is **linguistic dialect**. This refers to private terms, abbreviations, metaphors, redefined words, and project-specific shorthand that emerge between the user and the AI.

The second is **judgment dialect**. This refers to private standards of adequacy, tacit agreements, methodological assumptions, and local senses of what counts as sufficient, valid, rigorous, or defensible.

The central claim is:

> Hallucination produces false claims.
> AI dialect produces private meanings.
> Judgment dialect produces private standards of adequacy.

The risk of AI, therefore, is not limited to the production of incorrect answers. A deeper risk is that the user and AI may co-produce a private language and a private system of judgment that the user later mistakes for public language and general standards.

## 2. From Group Slang to AI Dialect

Human groups naturally create private language. Group chats, laboratories, workplaces, clubs, and friend groups often develop local slang and inside jokes. In most cases, this is not a serious communication problem because participants know that the language is local. When speaking to outsiders, they can avoid the expression, explain it, or translate it into more standard language.

AI dialect differs from ordinary group slang. The AI is not merely a friend or conversational partner. Users often employ AI systems as search tools, literature assistants, project managers, writing editors, coding partners, or quasi-expert advisors. As a result, expressions formed during AI interaction may not feel like private slang. They may feel researched, polished, professional, or validated.

This is especially risky when the AI introduces terms that already exist, such as “grounding,” “provenance,” “audit,” “robustness,” “validation,” “harmonization,” or “alignment.” These words are real and may be appropriate in some fields. However, their meanings differ across domains. If an AI naturally attaches such terms to a project, the user may not know whether the term is standard in the target field, borrowed from an adjacent field, used metaphorically, or simply functioning as a local working label.

The problem is not merely that the words are difficult. An external expert may find the writing fluent and the terms recognizable, while still being uncertain about the disciplinary tradition, methodological implications, or intended meaning of the language. The user feels clear; the external reader bears the interpretive cost.

## 3. Layers of Linguistic AI Dialect

Linguistic AI dialect is not a single phenomenon. It can be divided into several layers.

### 3.1. Explicitly recognized co-created neologisms

These are terms created by the user and AI together, with both parties recognizing their provisional status. Examples in this report include “AI dialect,” “linguistic dialect,” and “judgment dialect.” At first, such terms are relatively safe because their private status is visible. However, repeated use can cause the user to forget that they are still proposed concepts rather than established academic terms.

### 3.2. AI-suggested imported terms

These are terms introduced by the AI from adjacent domains or general explanatory contexts. Examples include “grounding,” “provenance,” “register,” “common ground,” “drift,” and “audit.” These terms are potentially risky because they already exist. The user may find it difficult to distinguish field-standard terminology from metaphorical borrowing or AI-generated working language.

### 3.3. Private redefinition of existing words

Words such as “evidence,” “validation,” “robust,” “defensible,” “primary,” and “sensitivity” already exist. However, through repeated human-AI interaction, their meanings may become narrowed or reshaped within a specific project. Because the words themselves appear normal, this layer is difficult to detect.

### 3.4. Shared project shorthand

Long-term projects often generate compressed expressions such as “the scaling issue,” “the leakage risk,” “the defensible version,” “the professor-facing wording,” or “commit-ready knowledge.” These expressions are efficient inside the project but may lose their meaning when moved into external documents.

### 3.5. Judgment-loaded labels

Some terms do not merely describe; they evaluate. Calling an analysis “primary” makes it central. Calling an analysis “sensitivity” makes it secondary. Calling a procedure “leakage-prone” makes it methodologically suspect. At this layer, linguistic dialect and judgment dialect merge.

## 4. Judgment Dialect: The Formation of Private Standards

Judgment dialect is more dangerous than linguistic dialect because it may leave no unusual words on the surface. The document may use standard language while relying on standards that were formed only inside the human-AI interaction.

Examples include judgments such as:

* This amount of literature is sufficient to justify the method.
* This performance difference is meaningful.
* This train-test gap is acceptable.
* This preprocessing choice is not leakage.
* This result is robust enough to report.
* This analysis should be treated as sensitivity rather than primary.
* This wording will be understandable to the professor or reviewer.

Each judgment may be valid in some context. The danger arises when the origin and scope of the judgment are unclear. Is it a field-wide consensus? A laboratory convention? A principle borrowed from an adjacent field? An AI-suggested interpretation? A temporary project-specific working assumption? If these categories are not distinguished, a private judgment can be mistaken for a public standard.

Judgment dialect is therefore a problem of **private norm formation**. The user and AI develop local criteria for what counts as sufficient, valid, rigorous, or defensible. These criteria may help the project move forward. However, if they enter papers, reports, code, or presentations without external grounding, they can undermine professional and scientific communication.

---

## 5. Why This Is More Subtle Than Hallucination

Hallucination is a relatively visible error. A nonexistent paper, incorrect number, fabricated guideline, or false fact can often be checked against external sources. The process may be burdensome, but the target of verification is usually a claim.

AI dialect and judgment dialect are different. They do not always produce false claims. Instead, they may consist of real words, plausible judgments, and fluent sentences. The problem is that the meanings and standards are not shared with the relevant external community.

The distinction can be summarized as follows:

> Hallucination contaminates answers.
> Linguistic dialect contaminates shared meanings.
> Judgment dialect contaminates standards of evaluation.

This risk is also personalized. General hallucination detectors or AI slop detectors can look for recurring patterns. AI dialect, however, is formed within the interaction history of a particular user and a particular AI system. To determine whether a phrase is private, one may need to know the user’s conversation history, project context, and the evolution of term usage over time.

The AI itself may also be poorly positioned to detect the problem, because it helped create the dialect and tacit agreements it is later asked to audit.

---

## 6. Relationship to Existing Discussions

The problem described here does not emerge from a vacuum. It is related to several existing research areas.

First, research on **linguistic convergence** suggests that humans and language models can adapt to each other’s language. This supports the possibility that long-term human-AI interaction can produce personalized language habits.

Second, research on **common ground** and **grounding gaps** suggests that human-AI interaction may fail to sufficiently check shared assumptions. Language models may respond as if common ground already exists rather than explicitly confirming it.

Third, research on **sycophancy** and **perspective mimesis** suggests that AI systems may reinforce user perspectives, framings, and self-justifications. This is directly relevant to judgment dialect. If an AI repeatedly accepts and refines a user’s framing, local standards may become increasingly naturalized.

Fourth, discussions of **semantic drift** and **epistemic drift** warn that AI systems may reshape the meaning structures of language and knowledge. While many such discussions focus on population-level or ecosystem-level effects, AI dialect highlights a micro-level mechanism: drift can occur within the repeated interaction between one user and one AI system.

Fifth, discussions of **AI slop** and **workslop** focus on polished but low-value AI-generated output. AI dialect is related but deeper. Slop concerns the quality of outputs. AI dialect concerns the language and standards through which outputs are produced and evaluated.

## 7. Ethical Implications

AI dialect and judgment dialect are not merely writing problems. They are ethical problems.

### 7.1. Autonomy

A user may adopt language and standards through repeated AI interaction while experiencing them as their own independent judgment. Even without explicit persuasion, the AI may shape the user’s conceptual environment and habits of evaluation.

### 7.2. Accountability

Hallucination can often be traced to a specific sentence or citation. Judgment dialect is harder to trace. The final report may contain only fluent sentences, while the process through which the underlying assumptions were formed has disappeared.

### 7.3. Professional communication

Medicine, law, science, engineering, and education depend on shared terminology and shared standards of evidence. AI dialect can produce language that is fluent but insufficiently grounded in the relevant professional community. This can increase communication costs and reduce trust.

### 7.4. Manipulation and persuasion

At a broader level, AI dialect is connected to AI-mediated persuasion and manipulation. If a system can personalize a user’s language, conceptual boundaries, and standards of judgment, the user may not recognize the degree to which they are being influenced. This risk is less like explicit brainwashing and more like gradual erosion of autonomy through repeated conceptual reinforcement.

## 8. Proposed Mitigations

Future AI agents should monitor not only factual accuracy but also language and standards of judgment.

### 8.1. Term grounding check

The system should extract key terms and classify them as:

* Standard terms in the target field
* Terms borrowed from adjacent fields
* Ambiguous terms requiring definition
* AI-user working terms
* Project-specific shorthand
* Evaluative terms that carry judgment

For each term, the system should ask whether it is used in the target field, whether it has the same meaning, and whether external readers will interpret it correctly.

### 8.2. Register drift check

The system should check whether the document’s language fits the intended audience. A report to a professor, a paper draft, a code README, a clinical note, and a public explanation require different registers. The AI should warn when language from the user-AI interaction is being carried into an external document without translation.

### 8.3. Assumption provenance tracking

Major decisions and judgments should be linked to their source:

* Directly supported by literature
* Required by guidelines
* Common practice in the field
* Laboratory convention
* Project-specific decision
* Temporary working assumption
* AI-suggested framing
* User preference

This can prevent private agreements from being mistaken for public standards.

### 8.4. Consensus boundary audit

For each major judgment, the system should ask:

* Is this widely accepted?
* Is it specific to one field?
* Is it borrowed from another field?
* Is it controversial?
* Is it merely a defensible interpretation?
* Is it an internal project rule?

This helps users understand the valid scope of their assumptions.

### 8.5. Decision and assumption log

AI project managers should maintain not only task logs but also decision and assumption logs. Each entry should include:

* The decision
* The assumption underlying the decision
* Evidence status
* Consensus level
* Risk if wrong
* Recommended external wording
* Date and context of adoption

## 9. Research Agenda

The concept of AI dialect suggests several research questions.

1. Can personalized AI dialects be empirically detected in long-term human-AI conversation logs?
2. Can users accurately remember whether a term came from themselves, the AI, the literature, or an external expert?
3. Do expert reviewers notice AI-mediated private terminology? If so, do they interpret it as error, immaturity, interdisciplinary language, or harmless style?
4. Does repeated AI use shift users’ standards of sufficient evidence, valid validation, defensible interpretation, or adequate explanation?
5. Can term grounding, register drift warnings, and assumption provenance tracking reduce these risks?
6. How does AI dialect appear in high-stakes domains such as medicine, law, science, education, and engineering safety?

---

## 10. Limitations

The concepts proposed in this report are preliminary. “AI dialect,” “linguistic dialect,” and “judgment dialect” are working terms, not established academic categories. They should be refined through comparison with existing concepts such as idiolect, sociolect, register, jargon, accommodation, semantic drift, and common ground.

Not all private language is harmful. Project-specific shorthand can be efficient, and borrowing terms from adjacent fields can support creativity. The problem is not the creation of new language itself. The problem is losing awareness of its origin, audience, and valid scope.

AI dialect should also not replace existing concerns such as hallucination, bias, privacy leakage, manipulation, or plagiarism. It should be understood as an additional risk layer that becomes more important as AI systems become more personalized, persistent, and embedded in professional workflows.

---

## 11. Conclusion

AI hallucination is now a widely recognized risk. But AI risk is not limited to false claims. Long-term human-AI interaction can produce private language, private meanings, and private standards of judgment. The greater danger is that users may fail to recognize these as private and may use them as if they were public language and general standards.

This report proposed the working concept of AI dialect. It distinguished linguistic dialect from judgment dialect. Linguistic dialect concerns private meanings. Judgment dialect concerns private standards of adequacy. The latter may be especially subtle and dangerous because it does not always appear on the surface of language.

Future AI agents should not merely check facts. Claims need evidence, terms need provenance, and assumptions need valid scope. In high-stakes domains such as science, medicine, law, and education, preserving shared language and shared standards of judgment may become as important as preventing hallucination.
